About this Project

In the summer and fall of 2013, I did some work for Parsons' own Prototyping, Education and Technology Lab (PETLab) on an update to their "Activate!" project. Activate! is a Web site and curriculum about learning to make games, with a focus on social issues. From the Web site: "The games on Activate! celebrate getting active and solving problems on your street, in your town, and around the world."

When I came to Activate!, the curriculum consisted of a series of written game design challenges, starting at a beginner level and increasing in sophistication, which students would take on using the no-programming-required application GameMaker.

My task was to update the curriculum using a new application: the (also) no-programming-required GameSalad. This involved "porting" the games from GameMaker to GameSalad (i.e. recreating them in the new application), writing new tutorials, and also (brand new to the curriculum) recording video versions of the tutorials. This page presents the results.

Before diving into the lessons, consider reading up on the basics of GameSalad.

(And here are some leftovers that I cut from the first tutorial.)

Some Notes

I was not involved in the creation of the original Activate! curriculum. I did not come up with the designs for these games, or build the original versions (or write the original tutorials); neither did I create the assets used in the games, such as the images. I have linked in each entry to the old curriculum's corresponding page, where you can find the original resources. I did write the new tutorials, and the video scripts, and I made the videos (so any hint of amateur videography is my fault and 100% accurate).

And, of course, I built the GameSalad versions of these games. I have reproduced the original GameMaker games as faithfully as I could, but GameSalad is a different program, and some differences were insurmountable and required design changes to the games. Mostly this had to do with the smaller screen size GameSalad imposed so the games would be playable in a Web browser. The larger levels of the GameMaker originals simply wouldn't work in the smaller layout, so I had to redesign the levels in Solarball, Smog Cloud Madness, Operation Green Clean, and Lights Off (in general this was a lot of fun, so there's that smog cloud's silver lining). Lights Off presented a special challenge and I wasn't able to finish creating the tutorial materials, which I discuss in the game's entry on this page. The whole porting experience was the genesis of an essay I wrote later for my Game Studies class, a critical study of GameSalad.

A Caveat

This Activate! update hasn't been tested or gone live (the unfinished Lights Off entry doesn't help), and the work I did on it belongs to PETLab. Many thanks to Colleen and John for granting me permission to present it here.

Play the game on GameSalad Arcade, then learn to make it yourself by downloading the resource files and working through the written tutorial and/or video tutorial...

Oh, wait, you can't because there's no video tutorial and the written one is only partially complete. Yeah, I didn't finish this entry. Lights Off turned out to be as monstrous as its hero's power-crazed neighbors, at least to recreate in GameSalad. This isn't entirely surprising--it is the most complex game of the curriculum--but the primary challenge had less to do with the game itself than with a big difference between GameMaker and GameSalad.

Put simply, in GameMaker you could align things in your game to a grid, making it easy to control placement and movement very precisely. GameSalad doesn't have that (or it didn't when I did this project; maybe it does now). Unfortunately, Lights Off was designed to rely on this feature; there is zero unnecessary space in the original version's level layouts. For example, the character sprites are 32px wide, and the paths down which they move are also 32px wide. Movement is restricted so the characters never get out of alignment with the paths. But in GameSalad, without that feature (and with a rather loose collision-detection system), reliably moving a 32px-wide character down a 32px-wide path is virtually impossible, because it's too easy to get even 1px out of alignment and get stuck. Consequently, I had to make all the paths significantly wider, and that necessitated completely redesigning the levels. The lack of a grid system also made it extremely tedious to lay out level elements with any degree of symmetry (making sure a path is perfectly straight, for example).

Ultimately I was able to finish the GameSalad version, which you can play in all its hard-earned glory using the link above. But I ran out of time to finish making the written and video tutorials. This really bothers me, but that's the situation. I've included here what I did produce for the written tutorial, which covers setting up the moving camera. The resource files folder includes the complete GameSalad project.
